### Entry 1 — ~10:00

**What I was trying to do:** Start filling in the VECTOR.md. Literally just begin.

**What happened:** Immediately didn't know where to start. The template has sections but no process — no "start here" or "talk through these questions." As a facilitator, I'd never hand someone a blank framework without a warm-up or a prompt sequence. I instinctively wanted to just talk about the project and have someone else map it to the sections. The template assumes you already know how to decompose your knowledge into these categories.

**Category:** opaque

**Raw thoughts:** The framework is structured for output, not for input. It tells you what the finished document looks like but not how to get the information out of your head and into it. A facilitator would design a process around this — the template alone isn't enough.

**Category:** question / assumption

But it felt like I was bolting something onto the template rather than the template anticipating it. For a facilitator, "who pays and why" is one of the first things you'd establish in a discovery session. The template skips it entirely. Is this a gap in the framework or am I supposed to fold it into Design Doctrine?

### Entry 4 — ~10:25

**What I was trying to do:** Write the User Archetype section.

**What happened:** The template implies a single archetype (singular heading, one paragraph in the Fictioneer example). But ACME's primary user spans a spectrum — early career vs senior researchers with different pain points and different relationships to the platform. Couldn't honestly collapse them into one paragraph without losing the distinction that matters.

**Category:** question

**Raw thoughts:** Should the template signal that multiple archetypes are valid? "User Archetype" (singular) subtly discourages it. Also noticed the whole Target User section was getting verbose. The Fictioneer example is punchy — every word earns its place. That conciseness isn't just style, it's functional: harder to misinterpret, faster for an agent to parse. The template doesn't say "keep it tight" but the example teaches you to. Question: should the framework explicitly guide on tone and density, or is learning from examples enough?

### Entry 5 — ~10:35

**What I was trying to do:** Reflect on why filling in each section felt harder than expected.

**What happened:** Realized I'm hand-crafting something that's likely designed to be machine-generated. Erika's workflow appears to be: do messy discovery → feed structured inputs into a process → VECTOR.md gets generated → human reviews and tweaks. I'm doing all three roles at once — facilitator, structuring engine, and writer. That compounds the friction: every section requires me to simultaneously recall, decide what's relevant, decide what format it should take, and write it concisely. A generated draft would let me just validate and correct.

**Category:** question

**Raw thoughts:** Separate from the "what goes where" friction — this is about the mode of creation. Hand-crafting a VECTOR.md may never be the intended workflow, but a facilitator reviewing a generated one would still need to understand what each section *should* contain and what "good" looks like. The generation removes the writing friction but not the comprehension friction. Both matter, but they're different problems. The template needs to serve both: people generating it programmatically (schema guidance) and people reading/reviewing it (section-level "what belongs here" hints).

### Entry 6 — ~10:50

**What happened:** The product is actually two products — a reading platform and a submission/editorial pipeline. Different users (or same user in different modes), different workflows, different teams. Trying to write one VECTOR.md for both muddied every section. Made the call to scope to the reading side only.

**Category:** question

**Raw thoughts:** The framework doesn't address scope boundaries. "What is one product?" seems obvious until you're a facilitator looking at a platform with multiple distinct surfaces. A publisher's reading experience and their submission pipeline could each justify their own VECTOR.md. The template assumes the boundaries are already drawn. For a facilitator, drawing those boundaries IS the work — and the framework gives no guidance on when to split vs. when to combine. This would come up constantly in real engagements.

### Entry 8 — ~11:10

**What I was trying to do:** Fill in Competitive Position.

**What happened:** Instinct was to reach for workshop artifacts — elevator pitch, value proposition canvas — rather than write from the head. These are the tools a facilitator uses to *produce* competitive positioning. The VECTOR.md wants the distilled output but doesn't acknowledge the artifacts that feed it. The Research Manifest section is designed to hold structured research, but it sits at the bottom like an appendix rather than being connected upstream to the sections it should inform. A facilitator would naturally think: "I'd run a workshop first, produce these artifacts, then distill the competitive position from them." The template's flow (Competitive Position → Research Manifest) implies write-first, reference-later — but the real workflow is the reverse.

**Category:** question / assumption

**Raw thoughts:** This is a facilitator-vs-builder gap. A builder can write competitive position from instinct because they live it daily. A facilitator reconstructs from evidence — workshops, canvases, interview syntheses. The framework privileges the builder's path. For facilitator adoption, the relationship between summary sections and the manifest needs to be explicit: the manifest feeds the sections, not the other way around.

### Entry 10 — ~11:25

**What I was trying to do:** Connecting the dots between entry 8 and the provenance observations in Todd's First Impressions.

**What happened:** Entry 8 flagged that the manifest doesn't connect upstream to the sections it feeds. But this is the same friction identified in the first impressions document: "The work that produced the JSON is invisible in the output." The competitive position friction (entry 8) and the business model friction (entry 3) are both symptoms of the same underlying gap — the VECTOR.md presents conclusions without showing provenance. Which artifact informed which section? What workshop produced this doctrine? The manifest registers files but doesn't link them to the sections they support. In an automated workflow, this friction is reduced — the system could generate sections *from* manifest artifacts and maintain those links. But even then, a human reviewing the output can't trace a section back to its source without that connection being explicit. That's the provenance gap: not just "where did this research come from?" but "which research produced *this specific section*?"

**Category:** question

**Raw thoughts:** This might be the single most important facilitator friction so far. Facilitators are trained to show their working — the journey from raw data to insight to recommendation. The VECTOR.md skips the journey and presents the destination. That works if you built it yourself and remember. It breaks if you're reviewing someone else's work, onboarding onto a project, or auditing an agent's decisions months later.

### Entry 11 — ~11:35

**What I was trying to do:** Reflect on whether I actually created this "from scratch."

**What happened:** Used the Fictioneer VECTOR.md as a structural reference throughout — copied the headings, the bold label patterns, the section flow, even the number of doctrine slots. The task says "from scratch" but in practice I pattern-matched heavily against the example. The content is original (pulled from memory of a real project) but the structure is borrowed. A facilitator would almost certainly do this — you'd look at the example before attempting your own. The question is whether that's a problem or just how frameworks get adopted. The template alone (empty sections with no example) would have been significantly harder.

**Category:** assumption

**Raw thoughts:** The task implied no example, but the example was right there in the references directory. Hard to unsee it. This might mean the framework needs the example to be usable — which is fine, but worth naming. A blank template without a completed example would likely produce worse results and more friction. The example is doing heavy lifting that the template structure alone can't do.

### Entry 12 — ~11:40

**What I was trying to do:** Decide how closely to follow the template's implied constraints.

**What happened:** Made several unchallenged calls throughout the exercise: scoped to one product surface when the template didn't tell me to; assumed I could have as many or as few design doctrine principles as the product needed (template had seven slots, I used six); assumed I could add fields that weren't in the template (Business model, Scope); assumed archetypes could be plural. None of these had guidance — I just did what felt right for the product.

**Category:** assumption

**Raw thoughts:** The template is more flexible than it looks, but it doesn't tell you that. The Fictioneer example has exactly seven doctrine principles, one archetype, and a fixed set of Product fields — which reads as prescriptive even if it isn't meant to be. A single line like "adapt sections to fit your product" would have saved the internal debate about whether I was allowed to deviate. Facilitators are used to adapting frameworks, but they also want to know the rules before they break them.
